## Source quality notes

- A01 through A04 and A10 through A11 are official OWASP project pages. The pages identify an edition but do not show a day-level publication date. The article should cite the edition and avoid inventing a precise date.
- A05 and A06 use the current MITRE CWE 4.20 entries. MITRE's 2026 news page records the 2026-04-30 release date for CWE 4.20.
- A07 and A12 are final NIST publications with stable DOI-backed records.
- A09 is an IETF Best Current Practice published through the RFC Editor. It is the current OAuth security BCP and updates RFC 6749, RFC 6750, and RFC 6819.
- A13 is a multi-agency primary guidance document. Do not turn its policy position into a product-performance claim.
- A14 supports package-to-version matching. The limitation about reachability is an explicit LyraShield editorial inference and must be labeled as such.
- A15 and A16 are first-party product and coverage sources. They establish LyraShield behavior only, not general industry facts.
- A17, A18, A22, and A23 are maintained OWASP Cheat Sheet Series pages. They are precise implementation guidance, not certification or proof that a control was applied correctly.
- A19 through A21 and A24 use the version-tagged ASVS 5.0.0 source rather than the moving `master` branch. Cite version-qualified section or requirement identifiers if the article later names an individual requirement.
- A25 is GitHub's first-party Actions guidance and supports GitHub-specific workflow claims. It should not be generalized to every CI provider without a provider-equivalent source.
- A26 is final NIST contingency-planning guidance. The article uses it to support tested restoration and recovery evidence, not a claim that one recovery design fits every application.

## Claims rejected or narrowed

- Reject any prevalence claim about how often AI-generated applications contain a specific vulnerability unless the article adds an original study with clear sampling and methodology.
- Reject claims that AI-generated code is inherently insecure. The defensible claim is that generated code needs the same explicit security boundaries and verification as other code, with additional attention to agent inputs and permissions when agents act on the repository or environment.
- Reject any statement that OWASP Top 10 coverage equals complete application testing.
- Reject any statement that a clean scan, score, or passing retest proves an application is secure.
- Reject any statement that LyraShield finds or fixes all 50 controls. Seven controls require retained human evidence.
- Reject automatic pull-request execution language. A Fix Proposal is reviewable and approval-bound; exact server-generated patch execution remains outside the public claim.
- Reject a claim that prompt injection can be fully prevented by a system prompt, retrieval, fine-tuning, or filtering.
